# Who Is Luoguihua (罗桂花)?

Luoguihua was my grandmother on my mother's side. She passed away five years ago while I was in Shanghai working on my master's degree. No one in my family told me at the time. The last time I saw her was about a year earlier, during winter break, when I returned to Changsha. She mentioned that her stomach had been feeling strange, but we all thought it was nothing serious.

Every time my cousins and I visited her, Luoguihua would make chicken soup for us. It's almost a family tradition that the older generation finds the best free-range chicken to cook nourishing soup for the younger ones. Even though the nutrition is rarely appreciated by the children. Throughout my childhood, many of my worst food memories involved different kinds of chicken soup—with astragalus, goji berries, or red dates—especially the ones from Luoguihua's kitchen. My mother had to chase me all around the house just to make me finish a big bowl.

But I was fortunate enough to have a real conversation with Luoguihua as an adult the last time I saw her.

We were at Orange Isle in Changsha, the famous island park that commemorates Mao Zedong with its giant sculpture of his young face. Like many good people in China, Luoguihua loves Mao.

I told her how nervous I was. My academic focus was Japan—its language and culture—and I wanted to move there in the future. At the same time, I was afraid that people back home would see me as a traitor.

Luoguihua knows a lot, so she gave me the simplest, clearest advice:

> "Don't worry about what other people think. Just do whatever you want."

It was the most heartfelt and straightforward support I have ever received from my people ever. Everyone else would tell me not to forget my country or its history, and to stay balanced so I wouldn't appear to lean too far toward Japan. Only Luoguihua told me to listen to myself and not to worry about other people's opinions.

Today, I work in Japan in international relations. My job sometimes requires me to deal with difficult political issues and even to push back against my own government's position when I believe it is being unnecessarily harsh toward Japan. Over the years, I have become much more open-minded. I have learned to understand different perspectives, and I am still learning every day.

But the words my late grandmother spoke to me have become an increasingly steady source of comfort. The further I go in my career—working through complex international issues and balancing the interests of different stakeholders—the more often I find myself thinking of her advice. Whenever I begin to doubt myself, I return to her words. They remind me to trust my own judgment, stay true to my convictions, and have the courage to become the person I truly want to be.

Looking back, that brief conversation on Orange Isle may have shaped my life far more than either of us could have imagined. And Luoguihua will continue to live on—in my memories, in my beliefs, and in all the love I carry for her in my heart.
